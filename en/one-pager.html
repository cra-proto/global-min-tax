Global Minimum Tax

Summary

To reduce the incentive for multinational enterprise (MNE) groups to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions and limit the erosion of the Canadian tax base, the Government of Canada introduced the global minimum tax (GMT).

The GMT is the result of the Budget 2022 proposal to implement Pillar Two along with a domestic minimum top-up tax that would apply to Canadian entities of MNEs that are within the scope of Pillar Two.

The GMT requires qualifying MNE groups to pay a minimum effective tax rate (ETR) on their profits in every jurisdiction in which they operate.

The GMT ensures a minimum ETR of 15% applies to profits of qualifying MNE groups for fiscal years that begin on or after December 31, 2023.

Members of qualifying MNE groups may have to register and file a return.

Source of the rules

Bill C-69, which contains the Global Minimum Tax Act, received royal assent on June 20, 2024.

The Act is based on the Global Anti-Base Erosion (GloBE) Model Rules, as well as the commentary and administrative guidance on the GloBE Model Rules. These references are approved by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) and Group of 20 (G20) Inclusive Framework on Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (the Inclusive Framework) and published by the OECD time to time.

Canada is one of the members of the Inclusive Framework that joined a two-pillar plan for international tax reform. Pillar Two of the two-pillar plan builds on the OECD/G20 Base Erosion and Profit Shifting project.

Members of the Inclusive Framework implementing Pillar Two are required to do so in a way that is consistent with the outcomes from the GloBE Model Rules, as well as the commentary and administrative guidance on the GloBE Model Rules.

What is the Global Minimum Tax Act

The Act implements key aspects of the Pillar Two Rules in Canada.

Part 2 of the Act implements aspects of the Pillar Two Income Inclusion Rule (IIR) found in Articles 2.1 to 2.3 of the GloBE Model Rules. As a global minimum tax it imposes liability for top-up tax on certain persons in respect of a qualifying MNE group.

Part 3 of the Act allows Canada to retain taxing rights on income sourced in Canada. As a domestic minimum top-up tax it ensures any qualifying MNE groups with Canadian entities will pay the minimum rate of 15% tax in Canada in respect of those entities.

Who must register and file

A qualifying MNE group that has €750 million in revenue or more reported in the consolidated financial statements of its ultimate parent entity (UPE) in at least two of the four fiscal years immediately preceding the particular fiscal year may be required to register and file with the CRA one or more of the following:

  • a GloBE information return (GIR)
  • a GMT return (Part 2 or Part 3)
  • a GMT notification return

One constituent entity or person may be appointed as the designated entity or person to file on behalf of all constituent entities or persons that must file with the CRA.

Who must file a GIR

The following constituent entities of a qualifying MNE group must file a GIR with the CRA:

  • the UPE, if:
    • the UPE is located in Canada
    • the MNE Group does not have a designated filing entity (DFE) located in Canada
  • the DFE, if:
    • it is located in Canada, or
  • each constituent entity located in Canada if:
    • there is no UPE or DFE located in Canada
    • there is no UPE or DFE outside Canada that filed the GIR with the CRA
    • there is no qualifying foreign filing entity (QFFE)
    • there is a QFFE, but the CRA notifies the constituent entities that it has not received the GIR from the QFFE’s jurisdiction

Who must file a GMT return

A person that is liable to pay tax under Part 2 or Part 3 of the Act must file a Part 2 or a Part 3 GMT return with the CRA.

Who must file a GMT notification form

If a QFFE is filing the GIR for the MNE group with the tax authority of the jurisdiction where it is located, each constituent entity located in Canada must file a GMT notification form with the CRA.
